## The best way to learn good UX is to experience bad UX

Design principles are abstract until you feel them. Readability, hierarchy, restraint — they're just words until you've squinted at neon green on magenta and wondered why your eyes are burning.

**That's what this piece is for.**

<KeyInsight title="The thesis">
Good UX is invisible. Bad UX is unforgettable. The contrast between them teaches more than any design system doc.
</KeyInsight>

## What just happened?

If you toggled to Bad UX, you probably noticed:

- **Your eyes had to work harder** — contrast, font choice, and visual noise compete for attention
- **The copy felt desperate** — caps, exclamation marks, and "limited time" language erode trust
- **The hierarchy collapsed** — everything screamed at once, so nothing landed

Good UX does the opposite. It gets out of the way. It lets you focus on the *content* — the offer, the value — instead of fighting the *container*.

<Callout>
The craft is in the restraint. Knowing what not to add is harder than knowing what to add.
</Callout>

## Why I built this

I've sat in too many reviews where someone says "we need more delight" or "make it pop" without a shared understanding of what good actually looks like. Words fail. Demos don't.

This little toggle exists so I can point at it and say: *that*. That's the difference. Now we're speaking the same language.

<SectionLabel>Take it with you</SectionLabel>

## What to do with this

Next time you're designing — or reviewing, or arguing about a design — ask: *Would this pass the Bad UX test?* If you flipped the colours, fonts, and copy to their worst versions, would the structure still hold? Would the hierarchy still be clear?

Good UX isn't about avoiding bad. It's about building something that *holds up* even when you stress-test it.
