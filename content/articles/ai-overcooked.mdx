## We're not just overwhelmed — we're overcooking

AI has often made me feel like we're playing Overcooked. Everything intensifies. Handoffs matter so much — and the skills of the people doing the handoffs matter to what we deliver to people. But there's a double meaning: we're literally overcooking. Moving so fast that quality burns. And we've stopped asking whether the recipe and time match the problem — whether we're still serving the right dishes.

I'm AI-fluent. I use it daily. And I'm exhausted. The pace of checking everything AI produces, at speed, is draining. Creating energises. Reviewing drains. We've shifted from one to the other without admitting the cost.

<ArticleIllustration name="ai-overcooked-01" alt="A calm kitchen before the rush — one chef, clear stations, manageable pace." caption="Before: a kitchen that breathes." />

## The productivity paradox

When individual tasks get faster, workload expands to fill the capacity. That's not a bug — it's human nature and organisational nature. We don't suddenly go home early. We handle six problems a day instead of one. We're not sizing up the recipe or the time for each one. A quick fry gets the same treatment as a slow roast. We're just cooking faster. The context-switching cost falls entirely on us. The AI doesn't get tired.

Software engineer Siddhant Khare wrote a viral essay about this: "AI fatigue is real and nobody talks about it." He builds AI agent infrastructure. He ships more code than ever. He feels more drained than ever. His job shifted from *creating* to *reviewing* — prompting, evaluating output, deciding if it's correct, fixing errors, re-prompting. A continuous review cycle. No flow state. Just decision fatigue.

<KeyInsight title="The backpressure problem">
AI generates faster than humans can verify. The human becomes the bottleneck. Teams have zero backpressure between fast AI production and slower human review.
</KeyInsight>

## Review fatigue and the rubber stamp

In human-in-the-loop AI, someone has to approve the outputs. When that someone faces dozens of similar recommendations — or hundreds of AI-generated variations — they stop genuinely evaluating. They rubber-stamp. We overcook. And we're not asking: was this the right dish to make? Did it need a slow roast or a quick fry? We've lost the moment to decide. Quality blind spots open up. That's not a people problem. It's a UX failure. We've designed systems that produce more than humans can meaningfully judge.

Heavy AI users report 45% higher burnout than those who rarely use it. We're optimising for output. Not for the people doing the handoffs.

## The boundary collapse

Here's the other thing: AI is erasing the lines between roles. PMs, engineers, and designers all produce similar artifacts now. A designer can ship production-ready code. An engineer can prototype UI. A PM can dive into backend logic. The time between "I know what we should build" and "here is a working prototype" compressed from weeks to hours. But who's deciding if we needed weeks or hours? Who's sizing the recipe to the problem? We're not recalibrating — we're just going faster.

That sounds like freedom. It also creates Mexican standoffs. Who's the Doer? The Decider? The Reviewer? Faster execution without clear ownership leads to incoherence. The handoffs matter more than ever. So do the people doing them.

<Callout>
We're optimising for output, not for the people doing the handoffs. And those handoffs are where everything breaks.
</Callout>

## What to do about it

I don't have a tidy fix. But I have a few things I'm holding onto:

- **Name it.** AI fatigue is real. Saying it out loud helps. You're not failing if you're tired. The system is asking too much.
- **Protect the handoffs.** If the bottleneck is human review, don't add more approvals. Redesign around decision rights. Auto-approve low-risk. Route high-stakes for focused human attention. Separate generation from decision-making.
- **Clarify ownership.** When everyone can produce, define who decides. Doer, Decider, Reviewer — for each output. Not job titles. Decision rights.
- **Size the recipe and time to the problem.** Not every problem needs a three-course meal. Some need a quick snack. Some need a slow roast. Before we cook, ask: what's the right dish? What's the right recipe? How much time does it deserve?

## The thesis, again

We're moving fast with AI — and we're overcooking. We're not sizing up the recipe and time for the right problems. We're optimising for output, not for the people doing the handoffs. Quality burns when we move too fast to check it. The people doing the handoffs matter more than ever. And we've stopped asking whether we're serving the right dishes.

If you've felt this — tired, even though you're "productive" — you're not alone. The kitchen is on fire. It's okay to say so.
